# Banned Language: Full List with Replacements

This is the working checklist. Run a search for each item before calling a draft done. The why-it-matters notes are for judgment calls; the rule is to avoid the word unless you can articulate why this case is different.

## Punctuation

| Banned | Replace with | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| `—` em-dash | period, comma, parentheses, colon, or rewrite | The single strongest LLM tell. Zero exceptions. |
| `–` en-dash as sentence punctuation | same as above | Same reason. Hyphens in compounds (`no-code`, `12-week`) are fine. |
| `;` semicolons in conversational prose | period | Allowed in lists, but rare in body copy. Most semicolons want to be periods. |
| `...` ellipses for trailing off | period or rewrite | An ellipsis in body prose almost always means the sentence was not finished. Finish it. |

## Sentence shapes that scream LLM

These are not single phrases but patterns. Watch for them.

### The parallel tricolon

> "It's faster, smarter, and more elegant."

Three parallel adjectives in a row. Almost always padding. Pick the one that matters and explain why it matters.

### The mirror

> "It's not about the tool. It's about the craft."

The "not about X, about Y" construction. When it lands once, fine. When you see it twice in one piece, it is a tell.

### The hedged contradiction

> "While AI offers immense potential, it also raises significant concerns."

Sentences that balance themselves into nothing. Pick a side or be specific about the tradeoff with concrete details.

### The "X is more than just Y" construction

> "Design is more than just pixels. It's about solving problems."

Almost always cliché. If you mean it, prove it with the actual content of the post, not with a thesis statement.

### The throat-clearing opener

> "Designing for AI is a complex topic that has gained significant attention in recent years."

Anything that frames the post as a topic before saying anything is a tell. Start with the point.

### The summary closer

> "By thoughtfully designing for these constraints, we can build experiences that truly serve our users."

The "by Xing, we can Y" closer. Cut the whole closing paragraph. The reader does not need to be told what they just read.

### The fake question opener

> "Have you ever wondered why some interfaces feel right while others fall flat?"

Cut. Start with the answer.

### The reader-implication move

> "We've all been there."

> "You know the feeling."

Both are filler. Cut.

## What to do when you find one

1. Read the sentence around the banned word.
2. Ask: what was the writer (or model) trying to say?
3. Rewrite the sentence with a concrete noun, a strong verb, or by deleting altogether.
4. If the only thing the sentence was carrying was the buzzword, the whole sentence probably wants to die.

## What is allowed

A few words show up on AI-banlists online but are fine in the right context. Do not over-correct.

- "Use" and "using" are fine. Do not replace with "employ" or "make use of".
- "Help", "let", "make easier" are fine.
- Specific UX terms are fine: heuristic, affordance, flow, friction, IA, pattern, primitive, density, hierarchy, spec, stub, scaffolding, retro, post-mortem.
- Specific AI/ML terms are fine: agent, model, prompt, context window, token, fine-tune, RAG, eval, hallucination, chain of thought, latency.
- Concrete craft language is fine: type stack, baseline grid, optical kerning, Bezier, viewport, breakpoint, micro-interaction.

The point is not to write in a deliberately small vocabulary. The point is to avoid the words that signal "machine-generated marketing copy" instead of "person who actually does this work".
